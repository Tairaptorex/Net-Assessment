How to use this resource

Below is a suggested lesson sequence for using this resource.


Lesson Sequence

  • Activate prior knowledge by asking students what they know about maraes.
  • Screen-share this website for students to view.
  • Read aloud (yourself or a student) the section A Little Background Information.
  • Ask students for definitions of highlighted words (e.g., Manaakitanga).
  • Fullscreen and watch the video.
  • Consolidate sections of the video by covering the following sections (Before Arrival to General Tips). You might ask students to read these sections aloud.
  • Play the content quiz with students. You might ask for student answers for each question.
  • If desired, students can play the quiz independently from within the student page.
  • Conclude the lesson with a discussion or by directing students to access the student page of the website at home.

A Little Background Information

What Is A Marae?

A marae is a very special place for Māori. It is where people come together to:

  • Enjoy their community
  • Welcome visitors
  • Celebrate
  • Learn
  • Remember and honour their ancestors
  • Meet, eat, and sleep!

Top tip: A marae is like a home, a school, a community centre, and a sacred place all in one!


The Importance Of Respect

When we visit a marae, we show Manaakitanga, kindness, and care.
That means:

  • Listening carefully
  • Following instructions
  • Being thoughtful about others
  • Remember that you are representing your family and our kura

Top tip: Even though a marae can feel big and open, it’s not a playground. It’s a special and important place, so we walk instead of running, and we use respectful voices.

Before Arriving

Be Ready

  • We will arrive on time or early
  • We will wear tidy, respectful clothes (if our school has a uniform, then that would be perfect!)

Be Prepared

The marae may have prepared:

  • A speaker
  • Someone to perform the karanga to call us onto the marae.

Top tip: Turn off or silence phones and devices. We show respect by giving the people on the marae our whole attention.

Going Onto A Marae

The Welcome

In Māori culture, there is a special way to welcome people onto a marae called a Pōhiri or Pōwhiri. The welcome has calls, speeches, songs, and sometimes challenges and haka. When someone has to speak for your group, it will be an adult male, so don’t worry about that. But, we’ll all be singing! The songs will be practised before we go.

Important: We cannot walk onto the marae by ourselves; we must wait to be welcomed onto the marae.

  • Wait to be told to go onto the marae
  • Walk together as a group (girls might be in one group and boys in another. Just follow the instructions you are given.)
  • Stay quiet and respectful

There may be a lot of talking or speeches, and much of this will be in te reo Māori. While people are speaking:

  • Sit still
  • Listen carefully
  • Don’t talk or move around
  • Don’t eat or drink
  • Don’t walk in front of speakers

After The Welcome

At the end of the welcome, we will form a line and greet the people of the marae. Be ready for:

  • Shaking hands (Harirū)
  • Pressing noses (Hongi)

Top tip: Just follow what your teacher is doing, watch what the others are doing, and enjoy meeting your hosts. Be respectful, though. This special greeting is important.


Time To Eat

Having kai together is an important part of the welcome and being on the marae.
Good manners are very important. Before eating:

  • Wait until a karakiais said
  • Let the elders go first
  • Wait to be told you can get your food
  • Remember, everyone needs to eat, so think of other people
  • Sit nicely

Top tip: Eat respectfully. Never sit on tables or pass food over someone’s head. This is impolite in Māori culture.

Entering The Meeting House

We will go inside the wharenui! This place is very beautiful and special!
Before entering:

  • It is very important to take off your shoes

After entering:

  • Walk calmly
  • Speak quietly
  • No food or drinks
  • Don’t step over people
  • Don’t sit on pillows

It’s all about being respectful.

Some General Tips

You may be asked to wash your hands:

  • After the welcome
  • Before eating
  • After certain activities

Washing our hands is all about keeping ourselves clean and being respectful. It is not just about our hands, it is also about spiritual cleansing in certain situations (such as when visiting an urupā)

Important: Don’t take photos or videos on the marae unless an elder from the marae gives you permission. Don’t take photos or videos of people or carvings on the marae, or of the inside of the wharenui.


There’s so much to remember!

Yes... and no.
It can feel like there is a lot to remember, and we can be afraid of making mistakes. But if we are respectful, kind, brave, and follow instructions, we’ll have an amazing time on the marae!

Our marae hosts will be looking forward to meeting us, getting to know us, and sharing their special marae with us.

Glossary

Haka
A traditional Māori posture dance involving movement, chanting, and rhythm. It is used to express pride, unity, and emotion, often performed during welcomes or important events.
Harirū
A handshake used during formal greetings on a marae, often following the hongi as part of welcoming visitors.
Hongi
A traditional Māori greeting where two people press their noses together, symbolising the sharing of breath and the joining of people.
Kai
Food. Sharing kai is an important part of hospitality and community life on a marae.
Karanga
A ceremonial call of welcome performed by women during a pōwhiri, guiding visitors onto the marae.
Karakia
A prayer or incantation used to open or close events, offer guidance, and acknowledge spiritual elements.
Kura
A school or place of learning.
Manaakitanga
The value of showing respect, generosity, and care for others, especially guests.
Pōwhiri
A formal welcome ceremony on a marae that includes speeches, karanga, waiata, and greetings.
Te Reo Māori
The Māori language, an official language of New Zealand and a taonga (treasure).
Urupā
A burial ground or cemetery, often located near a marae and treated with great respect.
Wharenui
The main meeting house on a marae, richly decorated and representing ancestors of the people.
